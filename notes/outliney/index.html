Outliney

Jun 2026

These notes are presented as collapsible outlines rather than flat documents. This page explains why.

▸

The problem with linear documents

Most written content assumes sequential reading. You start at the top and work your way down. But most readers don't do that. They scan, looking for the part that's relevant to them, and then they read that part closely.

A flat document makes scanning hard. You have to scroll through everything to find the section you want. Headers help, but they only go so far when the document is long.

▸

What an outline gives you

A collapsible outline shows the structure of the content at a glance. You can see every section and subsection without scrolling through the prose. Then you expand what interests you and skip what doesn't.

This changes the reading experience from "I have to get through this" to "I'll pick what's useful." For notes and reference material, that's a better fit than a linear narrative.

▸

Why this format for these notes

The notes on this site are meant to drive discussion, not to be read cover to cover. Each section is a position that can be engaged with independently. The outline format makes that easy: collapse everything, expand the section that provokes you, and go from there.

There is also a link toggle on some pages. Turning links off strips the references and lets you read the claims on their own terms. Turning them back on shows where the thinking came from. The content should hold up either way.

▸

How it works

Click any heading to collapse or expand it. That's it. No accounts, no JavaScript frameworks, no build step. Each page is a single HTML file with a few lines of CSS and a small script for the collapsible behavior.
